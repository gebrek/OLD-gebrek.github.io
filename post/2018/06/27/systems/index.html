Systems

Jacob Sonnenberg

2018/06/27

Tags: rough

It's difficult to observe a system, especially from inside of it. A system doesn't exist as a thing-in-itself per-say, but the relationships and interactions between things within the system. Observation of the system is especially difficult the more complex and larger it is, obviously, but to observe a system from within it as a complex actor is nearly impossible. As an actor within a system, conscious of the system, one has knowledge of what it is that is necessarily incomplete: a member cannot encompass the set.

Importantly, the best systems aren't dictated on high, but emergent. Perhaps not all the best systems, but the majority. This is because to be a good system, it must consider the requirements of its constituents. This aspect is unimportant in computer systems, because those are in fact dictated from on high, and the constituents are unconcerned because they have no needs. At least simple ones. Perhaps one may think of a distributed computer system as consisting of independent actors that have their own lives. This introduces a high degree of complexity to a system so it is no wonder why it isn't commonly implemented. It may make stronger systems though.

In many human systems consciousness of the system is a pre-requirement for participation in the system, at least to some degree. Democracy does not obviously function if the actors are not aware they're in a democracy.
